Children’s Coverage at Risk
The coming months could see major changes in children’s health coverage:
  • The Supreme Court will decide in King v. Burwell whether premium tax credits will be available in the 34 states that have not established their own health insurance marketplaces. The federal government has established marketplaces in those states.
  • Federal funding for the Children’s Health Insurance Program (CHIP) will end this October unless Congress extends it, eliminating separate CHIP programs in 36 states.
  • Currently, states must preserve eligibility standards for children until 2019. Proposed Congressional legislation would immediately let states reduce eligibility for Medicaid and CHIP to 138 percent FPL.

          About this graphic
          
        

        
These estimates are derived using the Urban Institute’s Health Insurance Policy Simulation Model–American Community Survey (HIPSM-ACS).  They extend an earlier series of Urban Institute reports on the impact of King v. Burwell.



For purposes of this analysis, we include 34 states as federally facilitated marketplace states, encompassing those where the federal government has taken on complete responsibility (19), those with explicit agreements with the federal government where the state takes on some responsibilities but not others (7), and states without explicit agreements but which have taken responsibility for plan management nonetheless (8). We do not include states that had created the legal framework for an SBM but for which technical problems led to use of the federal information technology system (HealthCare.gov).
